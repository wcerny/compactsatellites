Paper I: The Stellar Kinematics and Metallicites of
19 Extremely-Low-Mass Milky Way Satellites

(Cerny et al. 2026b, arxiv: )

Defining the Ultra-Faint Compact Satellites

We begin by defining the population of Ultra-Faint Compact Satellites (UFCSs) based on four selections:

(1) Azimuthally-averaged half-light radii $r_{1/2} < 15$ pc
(2) Absolute magnitudes $M_V > -3.5$ ($M_* < 4000 \, \rm M_{\odot}$)
(3) Surface Brightness $\mu_V > -24$ mag/arcsec$^2$
(4) Galactocentric positions outside of the disk plane ($|Z| > 5$ kpc)

This yielded a total known population of 29 UFCSs, in addition to two classical globular clusters.

A Dual-Hemisphere Spectroscopic Campaign:
215 Members across 19 UFCSs

We targeted 21 UFCSs using Keck/DEIMOS and Magellan/IMACS, recovering 215 spectroscopic members across 19 systems.

Key Result: the UFCSs are Kinematically Cold

We measure the velocity dispersions of 15 of our 19 UFCSs from samples of $\geq 5$ stars. In 10 of these 15 cases, we recover only upper limits, demonstrating that the UFCSs are kinematically cold systems. The remaining 5 cases displayed resolved dispersions up to $\sigma_v = 4$~km/s.

Key Result: the UFCS Population is Chemically Diverse and Extends Below $\rm [Fe/H] = -2.5$

We find that the UFCSs span a remarkably wide range in mean metallicity, ranging from $\rm -3.3 \lesssim [Fe/H] -3.3 \lesssim -0.8$. This is a larger range than that spanned by the entire confirmed Milky Way satellite galaxy population, and notably, extends well below the claimed globular cluster metallicity floor of $\rm [Fe/H] \approx -2.5$. This metallicity distribution strongly suggests the UFCSs are more than one class of object: higher-metallicity UFCSs are almost certainly star clusters, while lower-metallicity UFCSs are more ambiguous.

Dwarf Galaxies or Star Clusters?

Using our new kinematic and chemical measurements, paired with literat.re Our results demonstrate that gold-standard classifications based on stellar kinematics / dynamical masses are currently out of reach. Instead, mean metallicities and stellar population ages play a dominant role in determining UFCS classifications.

Key Result: A Growing Excess of Low-Mass Milky Way Halo Clusters

We highlight an apparent excess of low-mass MW halo star clusters, centered at $M_V \approx -1$ ($M_* \approx 400 \rm \ M_odot), that is clearly inconsistent with the lognormal tail of the MW GC luminosity function. While seen before, we demonstrate for the first time that this feature perists after removing the sample of UFCSs that are galaxy candidates. In other words, star cluster--galaxy confusion can no longer explain this feature of the MW satellite population.

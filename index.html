Paper I: The Stellar Kinematics and Metallicites of
19 Extremely-Low-Mass Milky Way Satellites

(Cerny et al. 2026b, arxiv: )

Defining the Ultra-Faint Compact Satellites

We begin by defining the population of Ultra-Faint Compact Satellites (UFCSs) based on four selections:

(1) Azimuthally-averaged half-light radii $r_{1/2} < 15$ pc
(2) Absolute magnitudes $M_V > -3.5$ ($M_* < 4000 \, \rm M_{\odot}$)
(3) Surface Brightness $\mu_V > -24$ mag/arcsec$^2$
(4) Galactocentric positions outside of the disk plane ($|Z| > 5$ kpc)

This yielded a total known population of 30 UFCSs, in addition to two classical globular clusters.

A Dual-Hemisphere Spectroscopic Campaign:
215 Stellar Members across 19 UFCSs

We targeted 19 UFCSs across both the northern sky and southern sky using Keck/DEIMOS and Magellan/IMACS (respectively), representing $\sim$2/3 the known UFCS population. Prior to the initiation of our census, only four UFCSs had published spectroscopic studies. Our census more than triples the sample!

Combining our new spectroscopy with ground-based photometry and astrometry from Gaia, we confirm between 3 and 35 spectroscopic member stars per UFCS (median of 9 per system). We present new measurements of the radial velocities and proper motions of these systems, setting the stage for analyses of their orbits.

Key Result: the UFCSs are Kinematically Cold

We measure the velocity dispersions of 15 of our 19 UFCSs from samples of $\geq 5$ stars. In 10 of these 15 cases, we recover only upper limits, demonstrating that the UFCSs are kinematically cold systems. The remaining 5 cases displayed resolved dispersions up to $\sigma_v = 4$~km/s.

Key Result: the UFCS Population is Chemically Diverse and Extends Below $\rm [Fe/H] = -2.5$

We measure the iron abundance ([Fe/H]) of at least one star in 18 UFCSs, more than tripling the sample of UFCSs with spectroscopic metallicities. We find that the UFCSs span a remarkably wide range in mean metallicity, ranging from $\rm -3.3 \lesssim [Fe/H] \lesssim -0.8$. This is a larger range than that spanned by the entire confirmed Milky Way satellite galaxy population, and notably, extends well below the claimed globular cluster metallicity floor of $\rm [Fe/H] \approx -2.5$. This metallicity distribution strongly suggests the UFCSs are more than one class of object: higher-metallicity UFCSs are almost certainly star clusters, while lower-metallicity UFCSs are more ambiguous.

Dwarf Galaxies or Star Clusters?

Using our new kinematic and chemical measurements, paired with literature age and mass segregation measurements, we attempt to classify all 19 UFCSs in our sample. We find that nearly half of our sample are clearly star clusters based on their higher metallicities and/or younger ages. We further identify 9 UFCS that are viable galaxy candidates, implying that \textit{up to} 50% of the UFCSs could be galaxies. Our results demonstrate that gold-standard classifications based on stellar kinematics / dynamical masses are currently out of reach. Instead, mean metallicities and stellar population ages play a dominant role in determining UFCS classifications.

Key Result: A Growing Excess of Low-Mass Milky Way Halo Clusters

We highlight an apparent excess of low-mass MW halo star clusters, centered at $M_V \approx -1$ ($M_* \approx 400 \rm \ M_{\odot}$), that is clearly inconsistent with the lognormal tail of the MW GC luminosity function. While seen before, we demonstrate for the first time that this feature perists after removing the sample of UFCSs that are galaxy candidates. In other words, star cluster--galaxy confusion can no longer explain this excess of low-luminosity GCs.
We speculate that this feature of the GCLF reflects the contribution of accreted low-mass star clusters from the LMC, SMC, and Sagittarius that have experienced gentler tidal fields throughout their lives.
